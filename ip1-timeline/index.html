Medical illustration dates as far back as the earliest human civilizations and the earliest illustrations were included in cave drawings as old as 3,000 B.C., depicting basic anatomy and injuries.

Anatomical drawings did not develop quickly, however, since some early civilizations, such as in Ancient China, included laws against dissections and thus illustrations were much more conceptual than they were accurate. The general view in the ancient world was that the human body was a sacred entity and human dissection was protested against well into the 19th century, hindering the progression of anatomical studies.

In medieval times, illustrations of anatomical systems, pathologies, and even illustrations of treatment methodologies began to emerge.

Medical illustrations naturally developed under the influence of historical, social, and technological developments through the ages. Those completed in the medieval period reflect an early reliance on classical teachings, rather than direct observation, so internal structures depicted are by no means accurate.

Early medical illustrations focused on art and the beauty and proportion of the human form over the science being represented. Medical illustrations were primarily featured as ornaments or portraiture on vases, columns, and tablets in early civilizations.

Around 400-300 B.C, medical illustration finally began to turn to instruction with Hippocrates' teachings, although the focus remained on animals. Then, in Hellenic Alexandria around 280 B.C.,

Herophilus, a Greek physician considered to be one of the earliest anatomists, initiated the practice of human dissection and the scientific foundations of medical illustration were laid.

Medical illustration did not progress much in Roman times, since the law still forbade human dissection. Some examples of illustrations popped up, though they mostly depicted battle or delivery scenes.

Galen, a renowned physician, surgeon, and philosopher in the Roman Empire was able to further anatomical and surgical knowledge to a limited extent, but his work was again based mainly on animal dissections.

The practice of medical illustration finally reached a breakthrough in the Renaissance period. This was largely thanks to human dissection finally being permitted and anatomists worked closely with artists to produce quality, accurate illustrations. The first publications of text juxtaposed with illustrations also came about in this period.

Jacopo Berengario da Carpi, an Italian physician, is credited with the first anatomical text with illustrations, his Isagoge breves, in 1522.

Andreas Vesalius followed this with the publication of his seven volume De humani corporis fabrica in 1543, a work known for such highly detailed illustrations of dissections that it is considered one of the most influential books on human anatomy. Vesalius is often referred to as the founder of modern human anatomy and his works were groundbreaking in the history of medical publishing.

Also in this period, Leonardo da Vinci, amongst all of his other accomplishments, became the first medical illustrator in a contemporary sense. His depictions of the human body melded scientific understanding with great artistic talent and he produced nearly 800 anatomical drawings based on his own dissections. Unfortunately, his anatomical work went unpublished within his lifetime and were eventually passed through Spain to England, where they were eventually brought to light in 1760. They are still owned by the British Crown today and remain in the Royal Library at Windsor Castle.

After the accomplishments of the Renaissance period, it became virtually inconceivable to describe anatomy solely with text. Numerous illustrated texts were produced over the next few centuries. Advancement was made especially possible due to lithography.

In 1725, Berhard Siegfried Albinius worked with artist and engraver Jan Wandelaar to produce two new books of muscular and skeletal anatomy, the Tabulae sceleti et musculorum corporis humani, which pursued new scientific accuracy within its engravings.

The 19th century also brought about advancements in printing that allowed for illustration work in a variety of media and especially in color.

Among the texts produced post-Renaissance, one of the best-known is Gray's Anatomy, from Henry Gray and Henry Vandyke Carter in 1858. The book has been widely influential for its showcasing of a variety of anatomy depiction techniques. It continues to be revised and republished to the present day; its latest edition, the 41st, came out in 2015.

Although its impact on medical illustration came later, it is also important to note the invention of the X-Ray in 1895 by German physicist Konrad Roentgen, since it made internal imaging a possibility.

It wasn't until the last century that the practice of medical illustration finally developed into a profession in its own right.

At the end of the 19th century, German Max Broedel came to America to join the John Hopkins Hospital as an artist. He employed new methods and created new techniques for illustrating and he went on to become known as the father of modern medical illustration. In 1911, he presided over the creation of the first academic department of medical illustration, which still exists and which served as a model for other medical illustration programs around the country.

Another notable artist of the century was Dr. Frank H. Netter, who progressed from freelance art during his medical training to the 1989 publication of Netter's Atlas of Human Anatomy and work with CIBA Pharmaceutical Company. In all, he produced thousands of illustrations and countless publications in his career and his works have become staples of medical education.

Hand-drawn and painted illustration still have their place even today, but the further development of photography and later digital imaging in the 19th and 20th centuries allowed for new techniques and possibilities in the practice.

Modern day medical illustrators can complete a three-year graduate program at a number of institutions in North America and Europe and can choose to specialize in both their medium and their subject matter. They produce material and serve as consultants and administrators within biocommunications.

In modern day practice, medical illustrations can take all sorts of forms, both hand-drawn and digital, and continue to be highly relevant and useful. Understanding of anatomical science continues to progress and medical illustrations are just as useful for educational purposes like models and textbooks, as they are for depicting new understandings in research and surgical techniques. Medical illustration is an ongoing practice and will continue to be as the pursuit of medicine continues.
