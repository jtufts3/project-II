FAQs

A list of common questions about medical illustration and a list of additional resources for further reading.

What recent developments have occurred in medical illustration in the new century?

A lot of emphasis is being given to computer graphics and all the potential behind digital imaging, especially as the technology for internal imaging continues to progress.

New discoveries are also in need of illustration, such as new surgical techniques or the results of newly published research, so medical illustrators are called upon to create visualizations for these as they come up.

One of the best examples of this is for the current coronavirus pandemic: when the virus went global, organizations like the CDC and WHO led the charge with the first illustrations of the virus which have served as models for other illustrations around the globe.

With all of the recent developments in digital imaging, do we still need the tradition of hand-drawn illustrations?

The answer is yes! Hand-drawn illustrations can at times be more costly and time-consuming, but the detail, precision, and quality of hand-drawn illustrations is hard to beat, so both methods are still very much in use.

What use is medical illustration beyond textbook illustrations? What else is it used for?

Medical illustration does have a lot of educational purpose, but it is very much a part of biocommunications in general. Medical illustration is a way to communicate scientific, medical concepts in a way that might be easier to understand at all levels, rather than just a block of text.

It is popularly used in textbooks, but also in terms of data visualization, video graphics, and 3D imaging. Patient education is a particularly important use, as is the illustration of sub-molecular processes, use by attorneys for clarification in personal injury and medical malpractice cases, and in computer simulations.

If we can take photographs of the human body, why do we still need medical illustration?

Medical illustration is important in conjunction with photography because it can be especially useful in depicting concepts we can't capture on camera, yet.

Internal imaging and microscopes have come a long way and can be incredibly powerful, but sometimes medical illustration is still the best way to depict what is happening in the body, especially at a sub-molecular level too small to be seen by cameras and microscopes.

What is medical illustration like as a profession?

Although it is a small profession, with fewer than 2,000 practitioners worldwide, medical illustrators work diversely as professional artists in biocommunications, working to promote education, research, public relations, and marketing in medicine.

The field changes rapidly as medicine, science, technology, and engineering continue to advance, so medical illustrators work to provide accurate visual communications in all these fields.

Medical illustrators often pursue graduate studies and many specialize in either subject matter, media, or specific targets in their work.
